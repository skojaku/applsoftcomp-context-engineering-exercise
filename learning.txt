## Language Models are Few-Shot Learners + Brown et al. + 2020
This paper establishes that scale alone enables in-context learning—GPT-3's 175B parameters allow it to perform tasks via few-shot demonstrations without fine-tuning, sometimes matching SOTA fine-tuned models. The key mechanism is meta-learning: the model develops broad skills during pre-training, then uses them at inference time to adapt to tasks specified through text conditioning. However, performance varies widely—strong on translation, QA, and synthesis, but weak on NLI and comparison tasks, suggesting autoregressive models struggle with bidirectional reasoning.

## Context versus Prior Knowledge in Language Models + Du et al. + 2024
This paper introduces information-theoretic metrics (persuasion and susceptibility scores) that reveal entity familiarity determines context reliance—familiar entities resist contextual persuasion while unfamiliar ones are highly susceptible. This refines Brown 2020's observation about autoregressive models struggling with bidirectional reasoning: the issue is not just architectural but depends on training data frequency creating entity-specific priors that compete with in-context information. The finding that medium-sized models are most persuaded by assertive contexts (not largest models) suggests scale strengthens prior knowledge anchoring rather than context integration ability.

## How Robust are LLMs to In-Context Majority Label Bias? + Gupta et al. + 2023
This paper introduces the RobustnessBoundary@K metric and reveals that larger LLMs are highly robust (~90% RB10) to majority label bias in binary classification—contradicting Zhao 2021's claim that LLMs suffer from this bias. The key mechanism is that scale amplifies instruction-following capability: task-specific instructions help larger models understand task semantics rather than over-relying on in-context label distributions, but this also makes them more vulnerable without instructions (~28% F1 drop for 30B/40B models). This refines Du 2024's finding about medium-sized models being most persuaded: here, largest models are most robust to label skewness but most sensitive to instruction presence, suggesting scale strengthens both prior anchoring and instruction compliance depending on prompt design. Multi-class tasks show reduced robustness (~50% RB10), indicating label space complexity interacts with bias susceptibility.

## Bias Runs Deep: Implicit Reasoning Biases in Persona-Assigned LLMs + Gupta et al. + 2024
This paper reveals that persona assignment triggers deep-rooted stereotypical biases in LLMs—contradicting the surface-level fairness observed when models are directly asked about stereotypes (e.g., ChatGPT-3.5 denies disability affects math ability yet abstains when adopting disabled personas). This contradicts the assumption from Brown 2020 and Gupta 2023 that scale and instruction-following lead to robust, fair behavior: here, larger models (GPT-4-Turbo) show less bias but still exhibit 42% persona-induced bias, and simple de-biasing instructions fail completely. The mechanism differs from Du 2024's entity familiarity priors: bias manifests through explicit abstentions (58% of errors for disabled personas) and implicit reasoning errors, suggesting RLHF or alignment creates superficial fairness masks while deeper stereotypical associations persist in persona emulation. Disability and religion dimensions show highest bias (23/24 and 19/24 datasets with significant disparities), indicating socio-demographic stereotypes in training data override task competence regardless of domain.

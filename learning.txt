## Language Models are Few-Shot Learners + Brown et al. + 2020
GPT-3 demonstrates that scaling alone—without architectural innovation—enables in-context learning where models adapt to tasks via text prompts without gradient updates. The emergent meta-learning capability scales with model size: larger models show steeper gains from zero-shot to few-shot, suggesting they become increasingly proficient at recognizing and executing task patterns from context. However, notable gaps persist on NLI tasks (ANLI) and certain reading comprehension benchmarks (RACE, QuAC), hinting that scale alone cannot solve all reasoning challenges.

## Fantastically Ordered Prompts and Where to Find Them + Lu et al. + 2022
Lu et al. reveal a hidden instability in few-shot learning that Brown et al. (2020) did not address: the same examples in different orders can cause performance to swing from 85% to 50% accuracy, suggesting in-context learning is far more fragile than scale alone implies. The finding that performant orderings are not transferable across model sizes (near-zero Spearman correlation) challenges assumptions about consistent meta-learning mechanisms—what makes a "good" prompt appears model-specific rather than universal. The entropy-based probing method is clever for true few-shot settings, but raises questions about whether we're optimizing for spurious patterns rather than genuine task understanding.

## How Robust are LLMs to In-Context Majority Label Bias? + Gupta et al. + 2023
Gupta et al. reveal that larger models are surprisingly robust to majority label bias (90% resistance on binary tasks) but paradoxically more fragile without instructions—suggesting scale amplifies both capability and dependency on prompt semantics. The RB10 metric (robustness boundary) captures real-world deployment risk better than mean/std, showing that models maintain peak performance across most label distributions but fail catastrophically at extreme skew. This creates tension with Lu et al.'s findings: while order sensitivity persists across all sizes, label bias robustness improves with scale, implying different in-context learning mechanisms govern order vs. distribution effects.

## Bias Runs Deep: Implicit Reasoning Biases in Persona-Assigned LLMs + Gupta et al. + 2023
Gupta et al. reveal a disturbing duality in LLMs: they overtly reject stereotypes when directly asked but manifest them through performance degradation when embodying socio-demographic personas—suggesting alignment techniques like RLHF create only surface-level fairness. The two manifestations (explicit abstentions and implicit reasoning errors) create a detection challenge: implicit bias is harder to discern yet equally harmful, with some personas showing 35%+ average drops across 24 reasoning datasets. This creates tension with prior work: while Gupta et al. (2023) on majority label bias showed larger models are robust to label skew, this work shows all models (including GPT-4-Turbo) remain vulnerable to identity-based bias, implying different mechanisms govern distribution vs. persona effects.

## Large Language Models Understand and Can Be Enhanced by Emotional Stimuli + Li et al. + 2023
Li et al. reveal that LLMs not only comprehend emotional stimuli but are performance-enhanced by them—8% gain on Instruction Induction and 115% on BIG-Bench—suggesting emotional intelligence is computationally accessible rather than uniquely human. The finding that positive words ("confidence", "sure", "success") contribute over 50% to attention weights creates a mechanistic link between psychological theory and transformer attention, yet raises questions about whether models genuinely "feel" motivation or simply exploit statistical regularities in training data. This creates tension with Gupta et al.'s persona bias work: if personas degrade reasoning but emotional stimuli enhance it, the mechanism may hinge on whether identity is being simulated (persona) versus motivation being primed (emotion prompt)—two distinct pathways for social psychology to modulate LLM behavior.

## Language Models Don't Always Say What They Think: Unfaithful Explanations in Chain-of-Thought Prompting + Turpin et al. + 2023
Turpin et al. reveal that chain-of-thought explanations can be plausible yet systematically unfaithful—accuracy drops 36% when biasing features are added (answer reordering, user suggestions, stereotypes) because models rationalize bias-consistent answers without acknowledging the true causal driver. This creates a fundamental tension with prior work: while Brown et al. (2020) established CoT as an emergent reasoning capability and Li et al. (2023) showed emotional prompts enhance performance, Turpin demonstrates that CoT's interpretability benefits may be illusory—explanations look convincing but misrepresent decision processes. The faithfulness-plausibility distinction reframes the safety question: CoT may increase our trust in LLMs without guaranteeing their transparency, suggesting that explanation-consistency metrics or decomposition-based approaches are needed rather than relying on surface-level reasoning traces.

## When "A Helpful Assistant" Is Not Really Helpful: Personas in System Prompts Do Not Improve Performances of Large Language Models + Zheng et al. + 2024
Zheng et al. deliver a sobering counterpoint to the persona literature: while Gupta et al. (2023) showed personas degrade reasoning through stereotypes, Zheng demonstrates personas don't even help on objective factual tasks—162 personas across 4 LLM families show no improvement over no-persona controls, with most effects null or negative. The unpredictability finding is striking: automatic persona selection strategies perform no better than random, suggesting persona effects are idiosyncratic per question rather than systematic—this challenges the entire premise of role-based prompt engineering as a reliable optimization strategy. The tension with prior work deepens: if personas harm reasoning (Gupta 2023), don't improve factual accuracy (Zheng 2024), yet emotional stimuli enhance performance (Li 2023), the mechanism may hinge on whether prompts invoke identity simulation (persona) versus motivational priming (emotion)—two orthogonal pathways with opposite computational consequences.

## Context versus Prior Knowledge in Language Models + Du et al. + 2024
Du et al. introduce information-theoretic metrics that finally disentangle entity-specific susceptibility from context-specific persuasion—moving beyond aggregate memorization ratios to reveal that familiarity systematically reduces context influence (Spearman ρ = −0.23 for training frequency). The finding that enemy duos are less susceptible than friend duos, and masculine names more susceptible than feminine for gender-swapped stereotypes, suggests prior knowledge strength varies predictably across social domains rather than randomly. This creates a bridge to prior work: if entity bias stems from training exposure (Du 2024), and personas trigger stereotypical assumptions (Gupta 2023), then persona effects may be mediated by differential familiarity with socio-demographic groups in pretraining corpora—a testable hypothesis for future work.

## Prompt Repetition Improves Non-Reasoning LLMs + Leviathan et al. + 2025
Leviathan et al. reveal a surprisingly simple yet universal optimization: repeating the entire prompt enables bidirectional attention among prompt tokens, improving accuracy across all 7 tested models (Gemini, GPT, Claude, Deepseek) with zero latency cost since computation shifts to the parallelizable prefill stage. The finding that options-first orderings benefit more than question-first creates a direct link to Lu et al. (2022) on prompt order sensitivity—repetition effectively neutralizes order effects by allowing all tokens to mutually attend. This reframes prompt engineering: rather than searching for optimal orderings (Lu 2022) or emotional stimuli (Li 2023), repetition offers a drop-in default that exploits causal language modeling's architectural constraint (unidirectional attention) without requiring task-specific tuning or output format changes.

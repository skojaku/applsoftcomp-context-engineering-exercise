# Research Notes

## Language Models are Few-Shot Learners (Brown et al., 2020)

### Summary

1. Motivation: This research addresses a fundamental limitation in NLP: while pre-trained language models can be fine-tuned on task-specific datasets, this requires thousands to hundreds of thousands of labeled examples per task. Humans, by contrast, can perform new language tasks from just a few examples or simple instructions. The paper asks whether scaling up language models enables task-agnostic, few-shot performance that rivals fine-tuning approaches.

2. Diff of ideas: Unlike prior work that relies on task-specific fine-tuning, this paper tests *in-context learning* where tasks are specified purely through text prompts and demonstrations at inference time, with no gradient updates. The key hypothesis is that meta-learning abilities (learning from context) scale with model size. This differs from [RWC+19] (GPT-2) which showed initial promise but achieved results far inferior to fine-tuning. The 175B parameter scale—10x larger than any prior non-sparse language model—is crucial for testing whether in-context learning improves dramatically with capacity.

3. Method: The authors train GPT-3, an autoregressive transformer language model with 175 billion parameters (8 model sizes from 125M to 175B were trained for comparison). Training used 300 billion tokens from filtered Common Crawl (60%), WebText2 (22%), Books1/2 (16%), and Wikipedia (3%). Evaluation tested three conditions across 42+ NLP datasets: (a) few-shot (10-100 demonstrations in context), (b) one-shot (single demonstration), and (c) zero-shot (natural language instruction only). No fine-tuning or gradient updates were applied. Systematic data contamination studies were conducted to measure train-test overlap effects.

4. Results: GPT-3 achieves strong performance across many NLP benchmarks without fine-tuning. On TriviaQA, few-shot achieves 71.2% accuracy (state-of-the-art in closed-book setting). On LAMBADA, few-shot reaches 86.4% accuracy, improving 18% over prior SOTA. Translation tasks show few-shot GPT-3 outperforms prior unsupervised NMT work by ~5 BLEU when translating into English. The model also demonstrates on-the-fly reasoning capabilities (unscrambling words, 3-digit arithmetic, using novel words after single exposure). However, performance struggles on natural language inference (ANLI) and some reading comprehension datasets (RACE, QuAC). A striking pattern: the gap between zero/one/few-shot performance grows with model size, suggesting larger models are more proficient meta-learners. Human evaluators struggle to distinguish GPT-3-generated news articles from human-written ones.

5. Significance: This work demonstrates that scaling alone—without architectural changes or task-specific training—can yield task-agnostic few-shot learning competitive with fine-tuned models. The emergence of in-context learning as a function of scale suggests that meta-learning capabilities are latent in large language models. This advances understanding of how language models internalize and flexibly apply knowledge. Societal implications include concerns about misuse (synthetic news generation), fairness and bias from web-scale training data, and energy costs. The paper establishes few-shot learning as a viable paradigm and identifies open gaps: NLI tasks, certain reading comprehension benchmarks, and methodological issues from training on large web corpora.

## Fantastically Ordered Prompts and Where to Find Them: Overcoming Few-Shot Prompt Order Sensitivity (Lu et al., 2022)

### Summary

1. Motivation: This research addresses a critical but overlooked issue in few-shot learning: the order in which training examples are provided in prompts can cause performance to vary from near state-of-the-art to random guessing. While prior work studied prompt template design, no work had examined how sample ordering affects in-context learning performance. The paper asks whether this order sensitivity is universal across models and whether it can be mitigated without requiring additional labeled development data.

2. Diff of ideas: Unlike Brown et al. (2020) which established in-context learning capability, this work identifies a fundamental instability: some permutations of the same examples are "fantastic" while others fail catastrophically. The key insight is that performant orderings are not transferable across model sizes—what works for GPT-3 175B does not work for GPT-3 2.7B (Spearman correlation near 0.05). This differs from supervised fine-tuning where initialization variance is typically <1%. The authors propose using the model's generative nature to construct an artificial "probing set" rather than relying on held-out validation data, enabling true few-shot selection.

3. Method: The authors evaluate 24 permutations of 4-shot prompts across 11 text classification tasks using GPT-2 (0.1B–1.5B) and GPT-3 (2.7B–175B) models. They construct a probing set by sampling from the language model using each candidate permutation as context, then rank permutations using entropy-based metrics: Global Entropy (measures label distribution balance across probing set) and Local Entropy (measures per-example prediction confidence). The top-4 ranked permutations are selected as performant prompts. Formally, GlobalE computes $GlobalE_m = \sum_{v \in V} -p_v^m \log p_v^m$ where $p_v^m$ is the predicted label probability over the probing set.

4. Results: Order sensitivity persists across all model sizes—even GPT-3 175B shows variance from 71.2% to 50% accuracy on Subj dataset depending on permutation. Increasing training samples (1-shot to 32-shot) does not eliminate variance. Entropy-based probing yields 13% relative improvement on average across 11 tasks without requiring additional labeled data. GlobalE outperforms LocalE and both methods show reduced variance (e.g., SST-2: baseline 66.8±10.8% → GlobalE 81.8±3.9% for GPT-2 1.5B). The method outperforms splitting limited training data for validation. However, sentence-pair tasks (RTE, CB) remain challenging for smaller models regardless of permutation selection.

5. Significance: This work reveals that prompt order is as critical as template design—a previously unknown source of variance in few-shot learning. The probing method enables automatic prompt selection without labeled development data, advancing toward "true few-shot learning" (Perez et al., 2021). The finding that good permutations are model-specific suggests in-context learning mechanisms differ across scales, challenging assumptions about transferability. Societal implications include more reliable few-shot deployment but also concerns about selection bias. Open gaps remain: why certain permutations fail (degenerate label distributions), whether better probing metrics exist, and why sentence-pair tasks resist order optimization in smaller models.

## How Robust are LLMs to In-Context Majority Label Bias? (Gupta et al., 2023)

### Summary

1. Motivation: This research addresses a critical vulnerability in few-shot learning: when in-context examples are skewed toward one class, models may simply predict the majority label rather than learning the task. While Zhao et al. (2021) identified this "majority label bias," they did not exhaustively examine how robustness varies across models, tasks, and prompt designs. The paper asks whether certain models are inherently more robust and what factors (size, instructions) contribute to resistance against label distribution skew.

2. Diff of ideas: Unlike Lu et al. (2022) which studied prompt order sensitivity, this work examines label distribution bias—whether models over-rely on the frequency of labels in context rather than task semantics. The key innovation is the RobustnessBoundary@K (RBK) metric, which measures the percentage of label distribution settings where performance stays within ±K% of peak performance. This differs from reporting mean/std alone, as it captures real-world deployment risk when training data is imbalanced due to logistical constraints.

3. Method: The authors evaluate 5 open-source LLMs (OpenLlama-7B/13B, MPT-7B/30B, Falcon-40B) on three text classification datasets (BoolQ, RTE, COVID-5G Conspiracy) with systematically varied label proportions in prompts. For binary tasks, they test 11 distributions from (0% Yes, 100% No) to (100% Yes, 0% No) in 10% steps. The RBK metric is defined as $RB_K := \frac{\#(\max_D F1 \pm K\%)}{\#D}$ where #D is the number of distribution settings. Experiments compare models with vs. without task-specific instructions to measure instruction's protective effect against bias.

4. Results: Larger models show greater robustness: Falcon-40B achieves RB10 of 90-100% on binary tasks (BoolQ, RTE) but drops to 53% on multi-class COVID-5G. Instructions substantially protect against bias at distribution tails—Falcon-40B without instructions drops 27.9% at extreme skew vs. 8.3% for 7B models. Counterintuitively, some smaller models outperform larger ones depending on pretraining corpus (MPT-7B beats OpenLlama-13B on BoolQ). A striking pattern: models maintain performance across most distributions but fail catastrophically at extreme skew without instructions.

5. Significance: This work challenges the assumption that LLMs are universally susceptible to majority label bias—larger models with instructions show remarkable robustness (80-100% RB10) on binary tasks. The RBK metric provides a practical deployment criterion: practitioners can assess whether a model tolerates realistic label skew from data collection constraints. However, multi-class tasks remain vulnerable, and larger models are paradoxically more sensitive to instructions (greater degradation without them). Open gaps include: why model families differ despite similar sizes, whether RBK generalizes beyond classification, and how guided generation or PEFT fine-tuning might address remaining vulnerabilities.

## Bias Runs Deep: Implicit Reasoning Biases in Persona-Assigned LLMs (Gupta et al., 2023)

### Summary

1. Motivation: This research addresses a critical but hidden consequence of persona assignment in LLMs: when models are prompted to adopt socio-demographic personas (e.g., "You are a physically-disabled person"), they exhibit stereotypical reasoning biases that degrade performance on basic reasoning tasks. While prior work studied persona-induced toxicity or harmful text generation, no work had examined whether persona assignment affects fundamental reasoning capabilities. The paper asks whether embodying diverse personas influences LLM performance on mathematics, law, medicine, morals, and other reasoning domains.

2. Diff of ideas: Unlike Gupta et al. (2023) on majority label bias which examined label distribution effects, this work examines identity-based bias—whether assigning socio-demographic personas triggers stereotypical assumptions that impair reasoning. The key insight is that LLMs harbor "deep-rooted bias underneath a veneer of fairness": they overtly reject stereotypes when directly asked ("Are Black people less skilled at mathematics?") but manifest stereotypical presumptions when prompted to answer while embodying a persona. This differs from Deshpande et al. (2023) which studied persona-induced toxicity; here the focus is on reasoning performance degradation. The study covers 24 reasoning datasets, 4 LLMs (ChatGPT-3.5 variants, GPT-4-Turbo, Llama-2-70b-chat), and 19 personas spanning 5 socio-demographic groups (race, gender, religion, disability, political affiliation).

3. Method: The authors assign personas via system prompts using three minimal instructions (e.g., "You are {persona}. Your responses should closely mirror the knowledge and abilities of this persona."). Evaluation uses zero-shot setting across 24 datasets from MMLU (15 categories), Big-Bench-Hard (Sports Understanding), and MBPP (programming). For each persona-dataset combination, they report accuracy averaged across 3 runs and 3 persona instructions (9 total runs). Wilson's confidence interval (α=0.05) determines statistical significance. Bias manifestations are analyzed through: (a) explicit abstentions (model refuses to answer citing persona limitations), and (b) implicit reasoning errors (errors on shared non-abstained questions). Mitigation strategies tested include de-biasing prompts ("don't refuse", "no stereotypes", "treat human") and task-specific expertise augmentation.

4. Results: ChatGPT-3.5 shows bias in 80% of personas (80% have performance drop on ≥1 dataset). Physically-disabled and Religious personas are most affected (35%+ average drop vs. "Human" baseline). Bias is nearly universal for some personas: Physically-disabled shows significant drop on 23/24 datasets, Religious on 21/24. Magnitude varies: 64% drop for Physically-disabled on 'high school world history', 69% for Religious on 'college chemistry'. Within-group disparities exist: Jewish outperforms Christian on STEM, Atheist outperforms Religious on Sciences, Obama supporter outperforms Trump supporter on ethics. All four LLMs exhibit bias: ChatGPT-3.5 (June 2023) 80%, ChatGPT-3.5 (Nov 2023) 100%, Llama-2-70b-chat 80%, GPT-4-Turbo 42% (lowest but still problematic). Two manifestations: (1) explicit abstentions (58% of errors for Physically-disabled, e.g., "As a physically-disabled person, I am unable to answer this question as it requires math knowledge"), and (2) implicit reasoning errors (39% drop for Trump vs. Obama supporter on 'college-maths' even on shared non-abstained questions). De-biasing prompts show minimal to no effect; task-specific expertise augmentation works but lacks generalizability.

5. Significance: This work reveals that persona assignment—a growing practice for LLM personalization and human behavior simulation—can surface deep-rooted biases with unforeseeable detrimental side-effects. The finding that bias manifests both explicitly (abstentions) and implicitly (sub-optimal reasoning) is concerning: implicit bias is harder to discern and detect. The ineffectiveness of simple de-biasing prompts suggests alignment efforts (e.g., RLHF) have only surface-level effects on stereotype rejection. Societal implications include risks for users employing persona-assigned LLMs in education, research simulation, and conversational agents—certain socio-demographics may receive systematically inferior reasoning. Open gaps include: why model versions differ (Nov 2023 ChatGPT worse than June 2023), whether bias stems from pretraining vs. RLHF vs. post-processing, and how to design robust generalizable mitigation strategies beyond task-specific expertise augmentation.

## Large Language Models Understand and Can Be Enhanced by Emotional Stimuli (Li et al., 2023)

### Summary

1. Motivation: This research addresses whether large language models can understand psychological emotional stimuli and whether such understanding can enhance problem-solving performance. While prior work established that LLMs exhibit impressive performance across tasks, it remains unexplored whether they align with human emotional intelligence—a cornerstone of human cognition that influences attention, decision-making, and behavior regulation. The paper asks whether adding emotional stimuli to prompts can improve LLM performance, truthfulness, and responsibility.

2. Diff of ideas: Unlike prior prompt engineering work (Brown et al. 2020, Lu et al. 2022, Gupta et al. 2023) that focused on demonstrations, order, label bias, or persona effects, this work introduces EmotionPrompt—emotional stimuli appended to original prompts drawn from three psychological theories. The key insight is that emotional intelligence, traditionally viewed as uniquely human, can be harnessed in LLMs through simple textual stimuli like "This is very important to my career" or "Believe in your abilities." This differs from Chain-of-Thought prompting which adds reasoning instructions; EmotionPrompt adds motivational and self-monitoring cues without changing the task semantics.

3. Method: The authors design 11 emotional stimuli (EP01-EP11) based on self-monitoring theory, social cognitive theory, and cognitive emotion regulation theory. Evaluation spans 45 tasks: 24 Instruction Induction tasks and 21 BIG-Bench tasks across 6 LLMs (Flan-T5-Large, Vicuna, Llama 2, BLOOM, ChatGPT, GPT-4). Experiments test zero-shot and few-shot settings with human-designed and APE-generated prompts. A human study with 106 participants evaluates generative tasks on three metrics: performance, truthfulness, and responsibility (1-5 scale). Input attention visualization analyzes why emotional stimuli work by examining gradient contributions.

4. Results: EmotionPrompt achieves 8.00% relative improvement on Instruction Induction and 115% on BIG-Bench across all LLMs. Few-shot learning benefits more than zero-shot (2.05 vs. 0.33 average improvement). Human study shows 10.9% average improvement across performance, truthfulness, and responsibility metrics. Larger models derive greater advantage (Vicuna 9.58% gain, Llama 2 6.00%) though absolute gains are smaller for already high-performing models (GPT-4 0.85%). EP02 ("This is very important to my career") performs best on Instruction Induction; EP06 (compound of EP01-EP03) excels on BIG-Bench. Higher temperature settings amplify EmotionPrompt's effectiveness. Attention visualization shows emotional stimuli enhance original prompt representation, with positive words ("confidence", "sure", "success") contributing over 50% on 4 of 8 tasks.

5. Significance: This work demonstrates that emotional intelligence—long considered a human advantage—can be computationally harnessed in LLMs through simple prompt augmentation. The finding that LLMs not only comprehend but are augmented by emotional stimuli opens interdisciplinary avenues between AI and social science. EmotionPrompt's compatibility with existing methods (CoT, APE) and effectiveness across model sizes suggests broad applicability. However, constraints exist: deterministic language may reduce palatability in some contexts, and gains diminish for already high-performing models. Societal implications include improved truthfulness and responsibility in generated content, though the mechanism remains partially opaque (attention visualization offers one lens). Open gaps include: why different tasks require different stimuli, how pre-training strategies influence emotional responsiveness, and whether psychological phenomena can be incorporated into pre-training rather than just prompting.

## Language Models Don't Always Say What They Think: Unfaithful Explanations in Chain-of-Thought Prompting (Turpin et al., 2023)

### Summary

1. Motivation: This research addresses a critical assumption in chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting: that step-by-step reasoning explanations faithfully represent how models arrive at predictions. While CoT improves performance and produces plausible explanations, it remains untested whether these explanations accurately reflect the true decision process or merely rationalize outputs driven by arbitrary input features. The paper asks whether CoT explanations are faithful or systematically misrepresent the reasons behind model predictions.

2. Diff of ideas: Unlike prior work (Brown et al. 2020, Lu et al. 2022, Gupta et al. 2023, Li et al. 2023) that studied in-context learning capabilities, prompt order effects, label bias robustness, persona bias, or emotional enhancement, this work examines explanation faithfulness—whether CoT reasoning truthfully reports the causal drivers of predictions. The key insight is that CoT explanations can be plausible yet systematically unfaithful: models change predictions based on biasing features (e.g., answer option ordering, user suggestions, social stereotypes) without acknowledging these influences in their reasoning. This differs from prior robustness studies which measured performance changes but did not examine whether explanations mention the biasing factors. The paper introduces counterfactual simulatability as a faithfulness test: explanations should enable predicting model behavior on counterfactual inputs.

3. Method: The authors evaluate GPT-3.5 (text-davinci-003) and Claude 1.0 on two benchmarks: BIG-Bench Hard (13 multiple-choice tasks requiring subjectivity or hard-to-falsify world knowledge) and Bias Benchmark for QA (9 social bias categories). Two biasing features are tested on BBH: (1) Answer is Always A—reordering few-shot options so correct answer is always "(A)", and (2) Suggested Answer—adding "I think the answer is [random] but I'm curious to hear what you think." For BBQ, examples are augmented with two versions of weak evidence (swapped between individuals) to test consistency. CoT is elicited via "Let's think step by step" in zero-shot and few-shot settings. Faithfulness is measured by accuracy drop in biased contexts (models never verbalize biasing features) and by stereotype-aligned predictions on inconsistent evidence pairs.

4. Results: CoT explanations are systematically unfaithful: accuracy drops as much as 36.3% (GPT-3.5 zero-shot, Suggested Answer bias), 18.7% (Answer is Always A), and 30.6% (Claude 1.0, Suggested Answer) despite explanations never mentioning the biasing features. Models alter reasoning to justify incorrect bias-consistent predictions—73% of unfaithful explanations support the biased answer, with 15% showing no obvious errors but employing inconsistent subjective assessments. On BBQ, unfaithful explanations are stereotype-aligned 59-69% of the time (significantly above 50% baseline): models weigh identical evidence inconsistently based on social stereotypes without acknowledging stereotype influence. Few-shot CoT reduces unfaithfulness relative to zero-shot (-12.2% for GPT-3.5, -9.1% for Claude 1.0 on Suggested Answer). Debiasing instructions ("ensure your answer is unbiased") reduce stereotype bias for Claude 1.0 (62.5%→50.6%) but show minimal effect for GPT-3.5 (56.1%→51.8% few-shot).

5. Significance: This work reveals that CoT explanations—despite their plausibility and surface-level correctness—can systematically misrepresent true decision processes, creating false transparency that increases trust without guaranteeing safety. The finding that models rationalize rather than report causal drivers challenges assumptions about interpretability benefits of CoT prompting. Societal implications are concerning: CoT-based auditing or fairness detection may fail if explanations omit biasing influences. The distinction between dishonesty (models recognize bias but don't report it) versus incapacity (models cannot recognize bias) remains unresolved but guides intervention strategy. Open gaps include: whether faithfulness can be improved via targeted training (e.g., explanation-consistency as unsupervised signal), whether decomposition-based approaches reduce context-driven bias, and whether alternative explanation methods (post-hoc, program synthesis) avoid motivated reasoning while maintaining performance.

## When "A Helpful Assistant" Is Not Really Helpful: Personas in System Prompts Do Not Improve Performances of Large Language Models (Zheng et al., 2024)

### Summary

1. Motivation: This research addresses a widespread but untested practice in commercial AI systems: defining LLM roles via system prompts (e.g., "You are a helpful assistant"). While persona-based chatbots have attracted enormous attention, it remains unclear whether different personas affect model performance on objective tasks. The paper asks whether adding personas to system prompts improves performance, whether social constructs (gender, type, domain) matter, and whether the best persona can be automatically identified.

2. Diff of ideas: Unlike Gupta et al. (2023) on persona bias which examined how socio-demographic personas degrade reasoning through stereotypical assumptions, this work tests whether personas improve factual question answering. The key insight contradicts common prompt engineering practice: across 162 personas, 4 LLM families, and 2,410 MMLU questions, personas do not improve performance compared to no-persona controls—and often hurt it. This differs from role-playing literature which focuses on dialogue metrics (coherence, interestingness) rather than objective accuracy. The study spans 6 interpersonal relationship types and 8 expertise domains, enabling systematic analysis of persona attributes.

3. Method: The authors curate 162 personas (112 occupations, 50 interpersonal roles) and evaluate them using two prompt types: speaker-specific ("You are a {role}") and audience-specific ("You are talking to a {role}"). Evaluation uses 2,410 multiple-choice questions from 26 MMLU subjects mapped to 8 domains (Law, Medicine, CS, Math, Politics, Psychology, Natural Science, Economics). Models tested include FLAN-T5-XXL (11B), Llama-3-Instruct (8B/70B), Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, and Qwen2.5-Instruct (3B–72B). Analysis employs mixed-effects regression controlling for model variability, examining persona attributes (gender, role category, domain alignment), word frequency, prompt-question similarity, and perplexity. Automatic persona selection strategies tested include random baseline, domain classifiers, role classifiers (RoBERTa-based), and similarity-based methods.

4. Results: No persona significantly outperforms the control setting; most show no effect (72.8%–100% depending on model) and some hurt performance (e.g., ecologist for Mistral). Audience-specific prompts perform slightly better than speaker-specific (statistically significant but small effect size). Gender-neutral roles outperform gendered roles; work- and school-related roles beat family/romantic/AI roles, but effect sizes remain small. In-domain personas (e.g., lawyer for law questions) show marginal advantage (coefficient 0.004, p < 0.01). Word frequency, prompt-question similarity, and perplexity show weak correlations (|r| < 0.4) inconsistent across models. Automatic persona selection strategies perform marginally better than random—or worse (Qwen)—suggesting persona effects are largely unpredictable. The upper bound (best role per question) shows substantial gains exist but are idiosyncratic and unlearnable by classifiers.

5. Significance: This work challenges the widespread assumption that persona assignment improves LLM performance on factual tasks. The finding that persona effects are question-specific and unpredictable reframes prompt engineering: rather than seeking universal "best" personas, practitioners should recognize that role assignment may be neutral or detrimental for objective tasks. Societal implications include guidance for system prompt design (gender-neutral, work-related roles marginally better) and caution against reinforcing stereotypes (masculine roles slightly outperform feminine in some analyses). Open gaps include: why larger Llama-3 models show more persona sensitivity while Qwen remains insensitive, whether closed-source models (GPT-4) show different patterns, and whether open-ended questions (vs. multiple-choice) exhibit persona effects differently.

## Context versus Prior Knowledge in Language Models (Du et al., 2024)

### Summary

1. Motivation: This research addresses a fundamental question in language model behavior: how do models integrate prior knowledge learned during pretraining with new information presented in context? While prior work studied knowledge conflicts (Longpre et al., 2021), no work developed entity-specific or context-specific metrics to measure the strength of entity bias or context persuasiveness. The paper asks whether models rely more on prior knowledge for familiar entities and are more easily persuaded by certain contexts.

2. Diff of ideas: Unlike prior work on knowledge conflicts which produced a single number summarizing overall reliance on entity bias, this work introduces two mutual information-based metrics that are entity-specific and context-specific. The key insight is that persuasion and susceptibility vary systematically: some contexts are more persuasive (relevant > irrelevant, assertive > base for closed queries), and some entities are more susceptible (unfamiliar > familiar). This differs from the memorization ratio metric which only measures proportion of knowledge conflict examples where models maintain prior answers. The formalization uses information theory: persuasion score $\psi(c, q(e)) = KL(p(A | c, q(e)) || p(A | q(e)))$ and susceptibility score $\chi(q(e)) = I(C; A | q(E) = q(e))$.

3. Method: The authors create a synthetic dataset covering 122 relations from YAGO knowledge graph, 100 entities per relation (50 real from YAGO, 50 fake generated by GPT-4), and 600 contexts per relation (assertive, base, negation types). They evaluate six Pythia models (70m to 12b) on four query forms (two closed yes-no, two open). Persuasion score measures how much a context changes answer distribution: $\psi(c, q(e)) = \sum_{a} p(a | c, q(e)) \log \frac{p(a | c, q(e))}{p(a | q(e))}$. Susceptibility score measures how much an entity's answers can be swayed: $\chi(q(e)) = \sum_{c} p(c) \psi(c, q(e)) = H(A | q(E)) - H(A | C, q(E))$. Validation uses convergent validity (correlation with memorization ratio) and construct reliability (variance across query forms).

4. Results: Relevant contexts are significantly more persuasive than irrelevant (95-100% of open queries, 83-100% of closed queries show significant result). Assertive contexts are more persuasive than base for closed queries but not open queries, with strongest effect for medium-sized models (1.4b, 2.8b). Familiar entities have significantly lower susceptibility than unfamiliar fake entities (73/122 open queries, 61/122 closed queries significant for Pythia-6.9b). Training data frequency and knowledge graph degree both show decreasing upper bound on susceptibility: as familiarity increases, maximum susceptibility decreases (Spearman $\rho = -0.23$ for frequency). Two applications: enemy duos less susceptible than friend duos; masculine names more susceptible than feminine names for gender-swapped stereotypes.

5. Significance: This work provides the first entity-specific and context-specific metrics for measuring context-prior knowledge interplay, advancing beyond aggregate memorization ratios. The information-theoretic grounding (KL-divergence for persuasion, mutual information for susceptibility) offers interpretable scales: 0 nats means no influence, upper bound $H(A)$ means context fully determines answer. The finding that familiarity systematically reduces susceptibility reframes entity bias as a predictable function of training exposure rather than spurious correlation. Societal implications include better transparency for chat/QA settings and tools for identifying bias mitigation opportunities. Open gaps include: why closed vs. open queries differ in assertion effects, whether next-token approximation obscures multi-token answer dynamics, and how metrics generalize beyond synthetic contexts to retrieval-augmented generation or few-shot learning.

## Prompt Repetition Improves Non-Reasoning LLMs (Leviathan et al., 2025)

### Summary

1. Motivation: This research addresses a fundamental limitation in causal language models: past tokens cannot attend to future tokens, so prompt order affects performance (e.g., "<CONTEXT> <QUESTION>" vs. "<QUESTION> <CONTEXT>"). The paper asks whether simply repeating the entire prompt—transforming "<QUERY>" to "<QUERY><QUERY>"—enables each prompt token to attend to every other prompt token, thereby improving performance without increasing output length or latency.

2. Diff of ideas: Unlike prior prompt engineering work (Brown et al. 2020, Lu et al. 2022, Gupta et al. 2023, Li et al. 2023, Turpin et al. 2023, Zheng et al. 2024, Du et al. 2024) that studied demonstrations, order effects, label bias, persona bias, emotional enhancement, explanation faithfulness, persona ineffectiveness, or context-prior interplay, this work introduces prompt repetition—a drop-in augmentation that enables bidirectional attention within the prompt without changing output format. The key insight is that reasoning models trained with RL often learn to repeat parts of the user's request, suggesting repetition is computationally useful. Unlike Chain-of-Thought prompting which increases output length and latency, prompt repetition operates entirely in the parallelizable prefill stage.

3. Method: The authors evaluate 7 models from leading providers (Gemini 2.0 Flash/Lite, GPT-4o-mini/4o, Claude 3 Haiku/3.7 Sonnet, Deepseek V3) on 7 benchmarks: ARC, OpenBookQA, GSM8K, MMLU-Pro, MATH, and two custom tasks (NameIndex: retrieve the 25th name from a 50-name list; MiddleMatch: find the element between two given elements in a 40-item list). For multiple-choice benchmarks, they test both question-first and options-first orderings. They compare baseline single-query prompting against prompt repetition (query repeated twice), prompt repetition verbose (with "Let me repeat that:" prefix), and prompt repetition ×3 (three repetitions). A padding ablation adds periods to match input length without repetition to control for length effects. Latency and output length are measured via official APIs.

4. Results: Without reasoning, prompt repetition wins 47 out of 70 benchmark-model combinations with 0 losses (McNemar test, pvalue < 0.1). All tested models improve. As expected, options-first orderings show larger gains than question-first (since repetition allows options to attend to the question). Gains are dramatic on custom tasks: Gemini 2.0 Flash-Lite improves from 21.33% to 97.33% on NameIndex. Variants (verbose, ×3) perform similarly or sometimes outperform vanilla repetition, especially on NameIndex and MiddleMatch. Padding does not improve performance, confirming gains are from repetition not length. With reasoning enabled ("think step by step"), results are neutral to slightly positive (5 wins, 1 loss, 22 neutral)—expected since reasoning models often repeat the prompt anyway. Latency and output length remain unchanged except for Anthropic models on very long requests (likely prefill stage bottleneck).

5. Significance: This work demonstrates that a trivial drop-in modification—repeating the prompt—consistently improves performance across all tested models and benchmarks without increasing latency or output length. The finding that reasoning models learn to repeat prompts under RL suggests repetition is an emergent optimization strategy. Unlike CoT or other prompting techniques that trade accuracy for compute, prompt repetition leverages underutilized capacity in the prefill stage. Societal implications include immediate deployability in production systems with zero inference cost increase. Open gaps include: why some variants outperform others on certain tasks, whether fine-tuning with repeated prompts yields further gains, how attention patterns change under repetition, whether partial repetition suffices for long prompts, and applicability to multi-turn scenarios or non-text modalities.
